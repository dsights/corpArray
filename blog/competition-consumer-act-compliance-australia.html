Competition and Consumer Act (CCA) Compliance in Australia: A Comprehensive Guide

Posted on August 5, 2025 by CorpArray

A fair and competitive marketplace is essential for a thriving economy, benefiting both businesses and consumers. In Australia, the primary legislation governing competition and fair trading is the Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth) (CCA). Enforced by the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC), the CCA aims to enhance the welfare of Australians through the promotion of competition and fair trading and provision for consumer protection.

For businesses, understanding and adhering to the CCA is critical. Breaches can lead to severe penalties, including substantial fines, reputational damage, and legal action. This comprehensive guide will delve into the key provisions of the CCA, outlining your obligations regarding anti-competitive conduct, fair trading, and consumer protection, and how proactive compliance can safeguard your business operations.

Understanding the CCA and the ACCC's Role

The CCA is a complex piece of legislation with two main parts:

  • Competition Provisions (Part IV): These provisions prohibit anti-competitive conduct that substantially lessens competition in a market.
  • Australian Consumer Law (ACL) (Schedule 2): This is a national law that applies uniformly across all states and territories, covering consumer protection and fair trading. (Note: We covered the ACL in more detail in a previous article, but its inclusion here highlights its integral role within the broader CCA framework).

The ACCC is the independent statutory authority responsible for enforcing the CCA. Its functions include:

  • Investigating Breaches: Investigating alleged breaches of competition and consumer law.
  • Taking Enforcement Action: Initiating legal proceedings, issuing infringement notices, and accepting enforceable undertakings.
  • Merger Review: Assessing proposed mergers and acquisitions to prevent anti-competitive outcomes.
  • Industry Regulation: Regulating specific industries (e.g., telecommunications, energy) to promote competition.
  • Consumer and Business Education: Providing guidance and information to help businesses comply and consumers understand their rights.

Key Competition Provisions (Part IV of the CCA)

Part IV of the CCA prohibits various forms of anti-competitive conduct:

1. Cartel Conduct (Division 1)

This is one of the most serious prohibitions under the CCA. Cartel conduct involves agreements between competitors to:

  • Fix Prices: Agreeing on prices for goods or services.
  • Share Markets: Dividing up customers or geographic areas.
  • Rig Bids: Colluding on tenders or bids.
  • Restrict Outputs: Limiting the amount of goods or services available.

Cartel conduct is illegal per se, meaning it is prohibited regardless of whether it actually lessens competition. It carries severe penalties, including criminal sanctions for individuals.

2. Misuse of Market Power (Section 46)

A corporation with a substantial degree of market power is prohibited from engaging in conduct that has the purpose, effect, or likely effect of substantially lessening competition in a market. This is not about having market power, but about misusing it to harm competition (e.g., predatory pricing, refusal to deal).

3. Exclusive Dealing (Section 47)

This occurs when one business imposes restrictions on another's freedom to deal with other businesses. It is prohibited if it has the purpose, effect, or likely effect of substantially lessening competition (e.g., requiring a retailer to only sell your products).

4. Resale Price Maintenance (Section 48)

This prohibits suppliers from dictating the price at which resellers must sell their products. It is illegal per se (e.g., a manufacturer telling a retailer they cannot sell below a certain price).

5. Mergers and Acquisitions (Section 50)

The CCA prohibits mergers or acquisitions that would have the effect or likely effect of substantially lessening competition in a substantial market in Australia. Businesses planning mergers must consider this provision and may seek informal merger review or formal clearance from the ACCC.

Key Consumer Protection Provisions (Australian Consumer Law - ACL)

While covered in detail previously, it's important to reiterate that the ACL forms Schedule 2 of the CCA. Key areas include:

  • Misleading or Deceptive Conduct (Section 18): Prohibits conduct that is misleading or deceptive or likely to mislead or deceive.
  • Unconscionable Conduct (Sections 20-22): Prohibits conduct that is harsh or oppressive.
  • Unfair Contract Terms (Part 2-3): Protects consumers from unfair terms in standard form contracts.
  • Consumer Guarantees (Part 3-2, Division 1): Provides automatic guarantees for goods and services (e.g., acceptable quality, fit for purpose).
  • Product Safety (Part 3-3): Covers mandatory safety standards, bans, and recalls.
  • Sales Practices (Part 3-1): Regulates various sales practices like unsolicited agreements and pyramid schemes.

Consequences of Non-Compliance with the CCA

Breaches of the CCA can lead to severe penalties and other adverse outcomes:

  • Substantial Pecuniary Penalties: For corporations, the maximum penalty for competition law breaches (e.g., cartel conduct) is the greater of $10 million, three times the value of the benefit obtained, or 10% of the annual turnover in the preceding 12 months. For individuals, penalties can be up to $500,000. ACL breaches also carry significant penalties (as detailed in our previous article).
  • Criminal Penalties: Individuals involved in serious cartel conduct can face up to 10 years imprisonment.
  • Disqualification Orders: Individuals can be disqualified from managing corporations.
  • Injunctions: Courts can issue orders to prevent further contravening conduct.
  • Divestiture Orders: In merger cases, the court can order the divestiture of shares or assets.
  • Damages: Parties who suffer loss or damage due to a breach can seek compensation.
  • Adverse Publicity Orders: Courts can order businesses to publish corrective advertising.
  • Reputational Damage: Public enforcement actions and media scrutiny can severely damage a business's reputation, leading to a loss of customer trust, investor confidence, and market share.

Best Practices for CCA Compliance

Proactive compliance with the CCA is essential for all businesses. Consider implementing the following best practices:

  • Develop a Competition and Consumer Law Compliance Program: Implement clear policies and procedures that address all relevant aspects of the CCA, including competition law, fair trading, and consumer protection.
  • Regular Training: Provide regular and comprehensive training to all relevant employees, particularly those in sales, marketing, procurement, and management, on their obligations under the CCA.
  • Review Marketing and Advertising Materials: Ensure all claims are accurate, substantiated, and not likely to mislead consumers.
  • Scrutinise Agreements with Competitors: Be extremely cautious about any discussions or agreements with competitors regarding pricing, market sharing, or bidding. Seek legal advice if unsure.
  • Assess Market Power: If your business has substantial market power, ensure your conduct does not have the purpose, effect, or likely effect of substantially lessening competition.
  • Review Supply Chain Agreements: Ensure that exclusive dealing or resale price maintenance clauses are compliant or have been authorised by the ACCC.
  • Due Diligence for Mergers and Acquisitions: Conduct thorough competition law due diligence before entering into any merger or acquisition, and consider engaging with the ACCC early.
  • Establish a Robust Complaints Handling Process: Effectively manage consumer complaints and provide remedies in accordance with consumer guarantees.
  • Seek Legal Advice: When in doubt, especially concerning complex commercial arrangements or potential anti-competitive conduct, seek expert legal advice.
